A liminal space is a beautiful thing. 

A space not meant to be inhabited, at least not permanently. Something better left as an experience rather than a permanence. 

Notable examples include:
- Airports (most notably for me, the tunnel connected the E and F gates at O’Hare, the tunnels connecting concourses at DFW, and the moving walkways under Dulles)
- The Crystal City Underground (or what’s left of it)
- Elevators
- Metro stations
- Nightclubs

There’s sort of a common theme here that underscores all of the above. Less of a theme maybe and more of a language, perhaps. Either way, it’s brutalism. 

Living in DC, there are prime examples of brutalism everywhere. According to Google, *”brutalism is a mid-20th-century architectural style characterized by raw, unadorned concrete (béton brut), massive blocky forms, and a focus on functionality and structural honesty.”* Most people associate brutalism with the Soviet Union, but it’s actually British. Regardless, to me, it’s really cool. The concrete monoliths of the 50s and 60s tower as powerful, imposing structures that were built in an era where things were meant to last. Maybe some day long after we’re gone they’ll be as widely regarded as some of the defining architecture of the 1900s and 2000s, just for being different. From the last century, there’s Art Deco, brutalism, midcentury modernism (a close-ish cousin of brutalism)…and nothing else that really sticks out to me. Feel free to call me out on that at kylenotbrandon@gmail.com if you feel differently.

Putting architecture aside, these are all places where you have a space for just a moment – it’s **yours** for an hour or two, but is it truly yours forever? I think that’s a cool concept. You could *think* about having an apartment in between B42 and B44. You could imagine what it’d be like to take a shower in a concrete cave with 30-foot ceilings. Yet you stay in an airport for 2-3 hours at most (unless you’re really unlucky). 

There is something unsettling about being alone in a liminal space. Part of that is the human condition. I know people who get scared being alone in any room. But liminal spaces are usually pretty cavernous, which exacerbates it. I get this weird feeling somewhere between awe, excitement, and anxiety in a liminal space. Even more so when it’s cold and concrete and brutalist. 

*(The other liminal space design that makes me feel this way is [Frutiger Aero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frutiger_Aero) . I’ll cover that design architecture later and get back on my soapbox to tell you that we’d all be happier with more Frutiger Aero.)*

Maybe one day I’ll strike it rich and I can build a brutalist house or a Frutiger Aero house or a house that reminds me of an airport. It’ll have big windows and natural light and I’ll be ever-so-slightly scared to live in it. But one can only dream. And dream I will.
