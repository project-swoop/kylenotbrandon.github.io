Metropolitan areas. Places where people gather to live, work, and play.

## What *is* a metropolitan area?

Well, the US defines it in a few ways. According to [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_area), there are 393 metropolitan statistical areas (MSAs) and 542 micropolitan statistical areas (μSAs) in the United States, forming a total of 935 core-based statistical areas. Beyond that, there are 184 combined statistical areas - essentially, either places where cities sprawl massively into other edge cities/exurbs, or places where cities formed independently and encroach on each other (like Dallas-Fort Worth or Washington-Baltimore). 

Not to say that CSAs aren't composed of their own MSAs and μSAs - because they are. For example, the Washington-Baltimore-Arlington DC-MD-VA-WV-PA (yes that is a lot) CSA is made up of **NINE** statistical areas. Holy crap!

But for the purposes of this blog, we'll mainly be looking at MSAs - statistical areas with cities of at least 50,000 where the biggest core city has a population over 50,000. At least I think that's how you define it if the Internet hasn't lied to me. 

And most of these MSAs are polycentric, which you can tell by the centers denoted in their official names. See *New York-Newark-Jersey City*, or...*Blacksburg-Christiansburg-Radford, Virginia*. (As you can see, some of these aren't really massive cities. But more on that to come.)

For the purposes of this blog, I'm drawing the line at MSAs with an estimated 1 million or more people as of the 2025 Census estimate. I feel pretty confident that most of these cities have a "downtown" that one would reasonably frequent, and suburbs that one would commute to/from. For the record, this is the top 56 MSAs in the nation, starting with New York-Newark-Jersey City and ending with Omaha. I'd wager each of these MSAs has a good-sized downtown among them, save for potentially Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario, California. (And maybe this is a bit of a bias for urban cores with tall buildings, but seriously - the Inland Empire is one massive suburb most of the time.)

So now...we can dive into the actual meat and potatoes of this blog (which is surely to be my longest yet out of three.)

## What is a suburb?

By definition, a suburb is a predominantly residential/mixed-use area within a metropolitan area which is within commuting distance of a large city. There are inner/first-ring suburbs, which are typically older and denser (and the first in line to be subsumed when urbanization expands), outer suburbs/exurbs (like Ashburn in the DC area), bedroom communities where people live and don't do much else (like Santee in San Diego), and satellite cities, which were their own thing but have been absorbed by a larger city (think Irvine, California). I had to Google an example for a satellite city there, I will admit.

So these are all suburbs. Makes sense, right? 

It should also make sense as to why suburbs are so appealing for (most) people. Want a yard and a dog? Suburb. Don't want to live in an apartment? Suburb. Want to live in a detached home? Suburb. Have an insatiable need to drive your car to do anything? Suburb. Want your kids to go to a "better school?" Suburb. Want to go on a power trip and be on the HOA board so you can police people's grass to the eighth of an inch? Suburb. Want to be separated from the "less fortunate?" Suburb. Watched too much mass media and scared you might get attacked unprovoked in broad daylight while crossing the street? Suburb. 

Anyways, I digress. 

As a side note, it *is* interesting how some suburbs seem super desirable out of nowhere and are far more expensive than living downtown but others are cheaper. Never got that.

Many of you probably grew up in a suburb. I know I grew up in multiple. Some because the whole city was a suburb and there **was** no urban area and others because that's just where we lived. It wasn't bad, overall. You can live a happy life anywhere. Although you're a touch weird if you live in the complete middle of nowhere (sorry to those who do). And if you're reading this from another country - first of all, how did you find this and second, please come visit the US and experience it. Even the mundane. 

But I also spent time living within actual city limits! Which raises the question...

## If you live in the city limits, are you still in the suburbs?

I'm inclined to say yes.

I lived in San Diego for many a year. I'd call it my hometown (while others would rather give me a hard time and disagree). But the notable thing about San Diego is the city limits are *massive*. You can live a 40 minute drive from downtown and still be in the city limits! Either way, still the suburbs. 

Other cities are more judicious about this. The City of Chicago is tighter on it, as an example. If Chicago had San Diego-style city limits, I'm pretty sure Elgin would be considered Chicago. Which is interesting, to say the least. 

So in this hypothetical world, could you live where Elgin is and say you're from Chicago? Can *I* really say I'm from San Diego? Is this all real? Is this a simulation? Well, in the final section of this blog, I break down the reasoning...

## How much of an identity are suburban residents allowed to *have?*

Comparison is often the thief of joy. Nobody should compare themselves to each other, especially not on the arbitrary stuff. Do I do this? Yes. Am I proud of it? No. But the culture around where someone's from and the policing around what people can/can't say is so, so, *so* weird. 

If you live in Arlington, VA and tell someone in Dallas you live in DC, they might remark that they've been to DC once and ask where you live. You may tell them, and they go, "Oh, that's not DC." You could live on the border of Maryland and DC and your mailing address be in Silver Spring and someone would say, "Oh, that's not DC," despite having been for about a cumulative fortnight. Someone who lives in DC might say, "That's not actually DC," despite you living right next to the Rosslyn Metro station - a 2-minute walk - and them living in that eastern corner of DC where the Costco is where the nearest Metro station is a 15 minute drive on a good day. 

Sooooooooooooooooooooooo...what is it? Because Timmy from Ashburn has been telling his friends at Northwestern he's from DC for a semester despite living an hour away, and Jack from Naperville gaslights every girl he meets who asks "Is it dangerous there?" when he tells them he's from Chicago. 

To be honest, and to underwhelm, it's really a judgment call. When someone says they're from one of the top 56 MSAs (or any notable city, I guess), maybe it's always worth asking what part. Because people usually do it for one of the three reasons below:

### 1. Convenience

*"Where are you from?"*

*"Oh, I'm from Houston!"*

*"Nice, I've heard it's nice there! What part are you from?"*

(Disclaimer: Houston is actually not that nice. You don't have to lie.)

*"Oh, I'm from Alvin, but I just say Houston because most people don't know where Alvin is."*

See? Innocuous. This is the bucket most folks fall into. No harm, no foul.

### 2. Pride

*"Where are you from?"*

*"I'm from Boston! The birthplace of America!"*

*"Nice, I've been once! Where in Boston?"*

*"I'm from Quincy, but it's all Boston! We all love the Celtics and the Red Sox!"*

(Note: If you meet someone who's ***REALLY*** passionate about Boston sports, consider running away. You might not want to associate with them.)

Some cities just have such an identity that it pervades the surrounding areas. Boston, Philly, Pittsburgh - you just feel the energy for miles around it. You should be proud of your city! I mean, usually. 

### 3. LARPing

*"Where are you from?"*

*"I'm from Chi-town! Chiraq! The Windy City, baby!"*

(If anyone calls Chicago Chiraq, they're probably not from Chicago or their heart's in the wrong place.)

*"That's cool, what part of the city are you from?"*

*"Naperville."*

*"Oh, that's pretty far away from Chicago I though-"*

*"BRO what do you mean? Naperville is Chicago! You're lucky my squad isn't here, or else you'd be leaving this bar in an ambulance! You're probably mad soft!"*

*"You didn't ask me where I'm from."*

*"You're probably from nowhere! You are not relevant!"*

*"Actually, I was born and raised on the Near West Side."*

*"Yeah whatever man! Just walk away, you don't know real Chicago like me!"*

Finally, we reach the long-awaited climax of this article with the third, final, and most acute type of person claiming they're from a city - the LARPer. For whatever odd reason, this person wants you to believe they saw struggles you can't imagine. They want you to believe they were shot in a dark alley before they could walk. And from some place of insecurity, they can't accept that anyone's more worthy of repping their city than them. I'll refrain from going into further detail, except that they're usually drunk and either A. in the process of getting kicked out of a bar or B. arguing with their girlfriend. 

Why do people do this? Insecurity. What do people do when you keep poking them on this? Usually fold pretty easily. Either way, interesting. 

## Conclusion

Suburbs are a necessary thing, and should be embraced even through their imperfections. Suburbs serve as the gateway for those looking to live at the foot of a larger city, and I can respect the folks who move out there - most do it for very pragmatic reasons. Would I do it? Not anytime soon. 

And people who live in their MSA should feel proud of their city - dare I say, even those who live in the larger CSA should be proud of the area they live in. Take pride in the place that lifts you up! And it really *is* no problem if you tell people the city you live in if you live in the suburbs when they ask. As all things in life - approach with humility, candor, and most importantly, humor.
